#+TITLE: Composing Games from Event Graphs
#+AUTHOR: Pancake
#+DATE: 2026-07-04
#+DESCRIPTION: We analyze an architecture that composes games from event-based interactions.

Traditional game architectures are typically built around an explicit
execution loop. Each frame, a fixed sequence of actions occur. In this
structure, game logic is expressed as functions that operate on stored
state. Objects encapsulate data, and systems mutate them in response
to time progression, often in ~Update()~ or ~Tick()~ cycles. This
brings forth a system which behaves /proactively/: The program
advances, and the world responds.

Today, we explore an alternative framing of games as networks of
reactive transformations. Instead of organizing the game around a
central cycle, we examine games in terms of how they listen for and
propagate changes through structured interactions. This shift
emphasises causality over sequencing: What triggers behaviour is as
important as the behaviour itself.

** Objects to Systems: OOP and ECS as Foundations

Traditional object-oriented design models games as collections of
objects that combine state and behaviour. A player may store position,
velocity, and health, and may expose methods such as ~move()~,
~takeDamage()~, ~consumeItem()~. In this model, interactions occur
through method dispatches between objects, often mediated by a central
game loop. While it classifies data intuitively, this approach
entangles behaviour and state in rigid class hierarchies, making
large-scale modifications and extensions difficult as the game grows.

Entity Component System (ECS) architectures emerge as a response to
these limitations. Instead of binding behaviour to objects, ECS
separate data into components and behaviour into systems. Entities
become lightweight identifiers that group components, while systems
operate on sets of components that match specific needs. For example,
a physics system might process all entities containing position,
velocity, and collision components, irrespective of the specific
entities at play.

This separation improves modularity by allowing new behaviour to be
introduced without modifying existing hierarchies. However, the
underlying execution model remains fundamentally the same: a proactive
loop that iterates over the system each frame, querying state and
applying transformations. While game loops are appealing for their
linearity and predictability, some game actions require reactivity
over proactivity. When working with networking, save systems, large
actions, story triggers, and similar subsystems, behaviour is often
expressed as a response to external or internal events rather than as
a part of a fixed cycle.

To address this, many engines introduce event-driven layers on top of
their core loop. Event buses, delegates, signals, and message systems
allow parts of a game to react to changes without being explicitly
polled each frame. A collision may emit an event, an input action may
trigger a callback, and a network message might be dispatch to
interested systems.

However, this shift is only partial. While events introduce
reactivity, they typically do so by attaching callbacks to objects or
broadcasting messages through a shared bus, rather than exposing a
structured representation of how interactions compose. The result is
that reactive behaviour exists at runtime, but its structure remains
implicit: It's distributed across subscribers and emitters, and cannot
be reasoned about as a part of a unified system. In practice, this
means that while ECS improves the modularity of data, the existing
reactive layers remain inadequate at providing equivalent clarity for
behaviour itself.

The problem is not an absence of behaviour, but the absence of
structure surrounding it. A callback describes what happens in
response to an event, but it does not reveal how the reaction relates
to the rest of the system. Dependencies remain implicit, making it
difficult to reason about causality, analyse execution, or compose
behaviour from smaller pieces. As games grow, the reactive topology
increasingly disappears into implementation details, rather than
existing as a representation that can be understood in its own
right. Thus, putting reactivity at the forefront requires revealing
the implicit structure of reactive interactions.

** Structured Reactivity

While traditional architectures build around a central loop, complex
games rely heavily on reactive extensions to handle systems that don't
cleanly fit frame-by-frame execution. This introduces a structural
requirement: If a game is fundamentally leaning to reactiveness, then
its primary unit of composition cannot be update steps, it must be the
event itself. Without events as first-class elements, reactivity is
always reconstructed indirectly through polling or scattered
callbacks, rather than expressed directly as structure.

Once events are treated as primary, a different pattern becomes
apparent. Every event originates from some source, and is consumed by
some set of reactions. An input action emits an event that is consumed
by a player movement controller. A collision emits an event that is
consumed by physics resolution and game rules. In both cases, events
don't exist in isolation, they exist as relations between emitters and
listeners.

This relation induces structure, emitters and listeners form directed
connections. With enough connections, the cluster of emitters and
listeners form a graph. Nodes represent events, and edges represent
listeners. What is typically implemented as an event bus or callback
system is, structurally, already a graph of interactions.

What this reveals isn't merely a visualisation of behaviour, but the
actual organisation of reactive logic. Each event participates in at
least one relationship of emission and consumption, and the system as
a whole is defined by these relationships. The graph is not an
interpretation of the system, it is the structure that the system
already implements.

From this perspective, a crucial distinction comes to light. Events
describe observations about the system, but they do not themselves
describe behaviour. Behaviour emerges from the transformations
relating one event to another. While an event may represent a
meaningful fact, it is the transformation that specifies how the
system responds to that fact. Consequently, the graph cannot be
decomposed into isolated events without losing the behaviour it
represents. The smallest unit that captures behaviour is therefore the
transformation connecting events.

Inspecting such a transformation more closely, each edge consumes an
incoming event, applies state-dependent logic, and emits one or more
resulting events. Unlike events, which merely denote observations,
these transformations encapsulate the behaviour of the system. They
are therefore the natural units from which larger reactive structures
are composed.

By now, the problem has become one of composition, not
representation. If a game is to be represented as a web of
interactions, then a usable framework must define primitives that
allow individual transformations to be assembled. Without these
primitives, the graph remains descriptive rather than constructive.

** An Algebra of Interactions

At this point, the description of a game as a graph of events is no
longer sufficient on its own. While the structure has become explicit,
it remains static. To resolve this, we shift our problem a final
time. Instead of asking about the structure of the whole of the game,
we will now emphasise the role of individual interactions, and study
the rules with which interactions compose.

Since graphs are complex, there isn't a single primitive of
composition. Instead, interactions behave under a small set of
fundamental operations.

*** Parallel composition

Some transformations operate on independent components of a composite
event. In this case, the separate transformations can be laid in
parallel.

For example, both ~GameTick~ and ~Event~ originate from the outside
world, but they are treated as causally independent. So, they follow
parallel segments on the graph.

*** Sequenced composition

When the output of one transformation matches the input of another,
transformations can be chained. This produces a causal link where a
long logical sequence is composed of intermediary stepping stones.

In the example game, the creation, registration, execution, and
resolution of game tasks are independent interactions. However, each
step of the interactions requires the prior, thus these interactions
are sequenced into one long branch that handles the whole of physics.

*** Branching (fan-out)

A single transformation can produce multiple downstream
interactions. A single event can therefore feed into multiple
independent causal paths. This expresses divergent behaviour.

To illustrate, the single ~Event~, a variant type of mouse events,
induces multiple actions relating to ball and surface creation.

*** Merging (fan-in)

Multiple distinct transformations may converge into a single
downstream interaction. In this case, different causal paths unify
into a shared consequence.

For instance, whether a ~GameTick~ triggers a physics response or not
doesn't influence the execution of physics. A new physics event might
not register, but execution happens every tick.

** Conclusion & What's Next?

So far, we've identified four operations of composition. These
operations are more than design choices. They correspond to
fundamental forms of composition studied in mathematics (readers
familiar with Category Theory may recognise them as operations on
arrows). Any graph-based representation of reactive behaviour
expresses these forms, either explicitly or implicitly.

However, the architecture hasn't reached a satisfying conclusion
yet. So far, we have ignored state, only focusing on events. Since the
event graph implies nothing about state dependencies, we can only
assume all state is effectively global. There is no need to worry,
however, as the second post in the series discusses how state can be
localized in a reactive system.

Moreover, readers who are observant may have noticed that the
~PhysicsExecute~ loops into itself. Our four operations don't yet
mention anything of loops. In fact, our operations perfectly construct
directed acyclic graphs (DAG), which doesn't match the accumulative
behaviours of games. To bridge the gap from acyclic graphs to cyclic
ones, we will introduce new primitives on the third post, also
concerning ourselves with the asynchronous job system implied in this
post.

To conclude, we began with familiar game architectures and observed that their
reactive subsystems already form an implicit graph. By treating that
graph as the primary representation rather than an implementation
detail, behaviour becomes something that can be composed algebraically
instead of orchestrated procedurally. In the next articles we extend
this foundation with localized state and cyclic interactions,
completing the transition from event graphs to a fully reactive
execution model.
